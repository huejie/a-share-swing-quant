## Design Context

### Target audience

Chinese individual A-share investors managing roughly RMB 100,000 to RMB 10,000,000. They are not institutional terminal users: they need a small number of actionable decisions, plain-language reasoning, strong risk visibility, and a product that works well on both desktop and mobile.

### Use cases

- Check the market regime and recommended gross exposure after the close.
- Review the current 3–5 stock portfolio and 2–3 reserve candidates.
- Understand why each stock is selected, what invalidates the thesis, and what action is due.
- Inspect theme lifecycle, market breadth, risk concentration, and portfolio drawdown.
- Review historical decisions, backtests, model versions, and simulated portfolio results.
- Configure capital size, eligible boards, risk limits, and notification preferences.

### Brand personality and tone

Calm, forensic, and protective. The interface should feel like a well-kept research ledger rather than a brokerage casino or a glowing “AI trading” dashboard. It is precise without being intimidating, opinionated without promising certainty, and candid about stale data and risk.

## Confirmed Design Brief

### 1. Feature Summary

Build a responsive decision-support application for an A-share medium-term swing system. The product turns market regime, themes, stock selection, position sizing, and risk controls into one explainable 3–5 stock model portfolio; the MVP supports research, backtesting, simulation, and daily recommendations but never places real orders.

### 2. Primary User Action

Within one minute, the user should be able to answer: “What should I hold tomorrow, at what size, why, and what would make me exit?”

### 3. Design Direction

- Voice words: calm, forensic, protective.
- Physical reference: a high-quality risk officer’s paper ledger with crisp annotations and stamped status marks.
- Tone: editorial/utilitarian, daylight-first, dense where comparison matters, spacious around decisions.
- Palette: warm paper-tinted neutrals with a restrained vermilion accent; green and red are semantic only and always paired with text or icons.
- Typography: a distinctive Chinese editorial sans stack for interface copy, with tabular numeric treatment for prices and returns; avoid fashionable AI-dashboard fonts and neon-on-dark aesthetics.
- Memorable element: a vertical “decision spine” connecting regime → themes → holdings → risk, showing exactly how each recommendation was derived.

### 4. Layout Strategy

- Desktop: compact left navigation, a broad decision canvas, and a narrow context rail for data freshness and risk alerts.
- Mobile: bottom navigation; the portfolio action list comes first, while derivation and evidence progressively disclose below it.
- Avoid card grids as the universal layout. Use ledgers, ruled sections, tables, timelines, and whitespace; reserve cards for actionable stock positions.
- Use a 4pt spacing system, fixed application type scale, and container-aware stock components.

### 5. Key States

- Healthy daily recommendation with 3, 4, or 5 holdings.
- Partial portfolio when only 1–2 stocks qualify, explicitly showing retained cash.
- No-trade/risk-off state.
- Data updating, stale data, provider failure, and partial-data states.
- First run without strategy results.
- Backtest running, completed, failed, and comparison states.
- Empty decision history and populated audit trail.
- Mobile, tablet, and wide desktop layouts.

### 6. Interaction Model

- Progressive disclosure: action and invalidation first; factor evidence and raw inputs on demand.
- Stock rows expand in place; model and data provenance use drawers or native dialogs.
- Filters persist in the URL where appropriate.
- Keyboard-visible focus, 44px touch targets, semantic controls, reduced-motion support.
- Motion is limited to transform and opacity, using short exponential ease-out transitions.

### 7. Content Requirements

- Chinese-first interface with concise, non-promotional language.
- Every recommendation includes action, target weight, entry state, thesis, invalidation, protective price, risk notes, model version, and data timestamp.
- Every chart has a textual summary and every status uses more than color.
- Always distinguish model output, simulated position, and real user holding.
- Persistent disclaimer: research and decision support, not guaranteed returns or automatic execution.
